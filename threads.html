About the Thread System

How I'm Specializing at Georgia Tech

Georgia Tech's ECE program uses a threaded curriculum: every student selects two threads, structured groupings of courses that define a depth of specialization relevant to their engineering goals.

I have chosen the Computing Hardware Emerging Architectures (CHEA) thread and the Digital Signal and System Design (DSSD) thread. Together they align with my interest in VLSI design and my goal of working at the intersection of computer architecture, chip design, and hardware-accelerated computing.

Deep Dive

Why VLSI Excites Me

Very Large Scale Integration is where abstract logic becomes physical reality. Taking a hardware description, synthesizing it through gates, placing and routing it onto silicon, then seeing it perform in a real circuit is something I find extraordinarily compelling.

In ECE 3150 and beyond I will learn to navigate tradeoffs between power consumption, propagation delay, and silicon area. These are the decisions that determine whether a chip fits in a phone, runs fast enough for real-time inference, or operates within a thermal budget in an embedded system.

My current Verilog work on the GT-8 Calculator is already building this foundation. Designing an 8-bit ALU from gate level up, then verifying it with a self-checking ModelSim testbench, is exactly the kind of thinking VLSI will require at higher complexity.

Why These Two Threads

The CHEA + DSSD Combination

CHEA teaches me how to design hardware from the transistor level up. DSSD teaches me how to reason about signals and the mathematical representations of the systems hardware must process. Together they make me a more complete hardware engineer.

A VLSI designer who understands DSP can make better architectural decisions about filter hardware, FFT accelerators, and data-path pipelines. The two threads reflect how real chip design works.

My longer-term interest in AI accelerators sits precisely at this intersection. Designing a chip that runs neural network inference efficiently requires both architectural depth from CHEA and an understanding of data flow from DSSD.
