ECE Threads

How I'm Specializing at Georgia Tech

Georgia Tech's ECE program is structured around a threading system where every student picks two areas to go deep in. The threads shape which electives you take, which research opportunities you qualify for, and what kind of engineer you come out as. I chose Computing Hardware Emerging Architectures (CHEA) and Digital Signal and System Design (DSSD).

CHEA is the natural home for someone who wants to design chips. It covers digital logic and Verilog all the way through VLSI layout and advanced processor architecture. DSSD adds the mathematical and systems-level foundation that I think serious hardware engineers need but don't always have. Understanding how signals flow through a system and how to reason about them mathematically makes you better at designing the hardware that processes those signals. The combination made sense to me from the start.

Deep Dive

Why VLSI Excites Me

There is something specific about VLSI that I find more compelling than most other areas of engineering. You start with logic that only exists as a description, a few lines of Verilog or a circuit schematic, and you end up with a physical structure on silicon that does exactly what you described. Every decision you make, how wide to size a transistor, how to route the clock tree, where to place cells to minimize wire length, has real physical consequences. Nothing gets hand-waved away.

My current work in ECE 2020 is the beginning of that. Designing the GT-8 Calculator ALU from gate level up through a full ripple carry adder, then writing a self-checking testbench that catches signed overflow and two's complement edge cases, is early practice for the discipline VLSI requires at much larger scales. When ECE 3150 comes around, I'll be working in Cadence Virtuoso with real layout tools. That trajectory is intentional.

Longer term I am drawn to how VLSI intersects with machine learning hardware. Designing inference accelerators, chips that run neural networks within tight power and area budgets, requires exactly this kind of work.

Why These Two

The CHEA + DSSD Combination

The way I think about it: CHEA teaches me how to build hardware and DSSD teaches me what that hardware has to handle. A chip designer who does not understand signal processing will eventually make architectural decisions that look correct on paper but fall apart when data actually flows through the system. Taking both threads together addresses that gap directly.

There is also a practical dimension. The roles I am most interested in after graduation, whether that is hardware verification at a semiconductor company, embedded systems at a robotics firm, or something closer to AI hardware, tend to sit exactly where these two areas overlap. Engineers who can reason about both the circuit and the algorithm running on it are harder to find and more useful.

I did not choose these threads because someone told me to. I chose them because they map to what I find genuinely interesting, and because they give me the most direct path toward the kind of work I actually want to do.

The Bigger Picture

VLSI and Hardware Intelligence

The problem I find most interesting right now is the gap between what neural networks can do algorithmically and what we can actually run on a chip. Training a large model in a data center is one problem. Running it in real time on a device with a 5-watt power budget and no internet connection is a different one entirely, and it is a hardware problem first and foremost.

CHEA gives me the circuit-level tools to design for power and performance. DSSD gives me the mathematical foundation for understanding the algorithms those circuits have to run. I am not ready to build an inference accelerator yet, but every course, project, and internship between now and graduation is moving me in that direction. That is not an accident.
